APS Guidelines & Responsibilities

Supplemental Instruction (SI) Program

Working document • Last updated: Jan 28, 2026

Purpose

This document converts a detailed APS/SI outline into clear guidelines, expectations, and repeatable responsibilities. It is designed to be a reference for planning, coaching, quality assurance, and program improvement.

Who This Is For

  • • APS leaders overseeing SI Leaders and supporting the SI program.
  • • SI Leaders who want a clear picture of what strong sessions look like and what deliverables are required.
  • • Program staff who need consistent language for training, observations, and performance coaching.

Program Principles

Students should leave sessions feeling more confident and more capable than when they arrived.
SI is for everyone - not only for students who are struggling. Regular attendance reduces exam stress.
Sessions must be active and collaborative: students practice, explain, and problem-solve (not just watch).
Documentation matters: journals/lesson plans and ERCs provide evidence for impact and coaching priorities.
Professionalism and privacy are non-negotiable: FERPA and mandatory reporting obligations apply.

SI Leader Guidelines

Session Design: Structure That Works

Aim for an engaging, back-and-forth flow:

  • Set learning objectives (what students will be able to do by the end).
  • Mini-instruction (short slide or explanation).
  • Activity/practice tied to that concept (worksheet, problems, prompts).
  • Checkpoint (quick questions, mini-polls, cold/warm calls, worked examples).
  • Repeat the cycle for each major concept.
  • End with a low-stakes quiz/game (e.g., Kahoot) to reinforce and diagnose gaps.

Recommended Toolkit

  • Slides: concise, focused, and aligned to class content.
  • In-session assignment/worksheet: practice for each concept you teach.
  • Kahoot (or similar): recap + engagement at the end of the session.
  • Whiteboard/annotated examples: model the thinking process, not just the final answer.

Engagement and Instruction Expectations

  • Use frequent checkpoints to confirm understanding ("Tell me the next step", "Why did we choose that?", "What would change if…?").
  • Coach students through the reasoning process rather than giving answers immediately.
  • Invite questions continuously and normalize confusion ("If this is fuzzy, that’s normal - let’s unpack it").
  • Keep pacing flexible: slow down when confusion is common; speed up when mastery is clear.
  • End with a quick recap of the learning objectives and what to practice next.

Required Deliverables

Journals / Lesson Plans (Before and After Each Session)

SI Leaders are expected to complete the journal/lesson plan BEFORE and AFTER each session. This is proof of planning and performance, and it guides APS coaching.

Before Session: What to Include

  • Session topic(s) and the specific course content being supported (chapter/sections).
  • Learning objectives (clear and measurable).
  • Your session plan: slides + activities + checkpoints + wrap-up (Kahoot/quiz).
  • How the plan connects to what students recently covered in lecture.
  • Materials you will use (worksheet link, slides link, Kahoot name).

After Session: What to Include

  • Attendance count and overall participation quality (brief).
  • The main questions students asked and where they struggled.
  • What strategies worked well (and what did not).
  • How you responded: did you guide students to the answer or give the answer? Provide an example.
  • Plan adjustments for the next session based on what you observed.

End-of-Record/Reporting: ERCs (Exam Report Cards)

ERCs compare exam performance of students who attended SI sessions versus those who did not. This supports program evaluation and helps communicate SI impact.

  • After an exam, download exam grades from Blackboard (gradebook).
  • Use the provided Excel file/template to separate: attendees vs non-attendees.
  • Calculate the exam averages and the difference between groups.
  • Record the result and save it according to program procedure.

Now and in this case, the Supplemental Instruction program stores everything on the SI Teams Channel.

Historical Benchmark (Messaging only)

Past records have shown SI attendees outperforming non-attendees by about 7% on exams.

Observations

Peer Observations

Last Month of Classes

Observer Responsibilities

  • Enter quietly and evaluate using the provided form (complete every section).
  • Look for clarity, pacing, engagement, and leader response.
  • Share feedback honestly and respectfully: suggest improvements.
  • Exchange observations (both leaders observe each other).

Learning Benefit

  • Identify techniques to adopt: style, pacing, structure, confidence.
  • Notice use of voice, body language, and group dynamics.

APS Observations

Early Semester Quality Check

Purpose: Catch issues early while there is still time to adjust and improve.

Common focus areas: Attendance procedures, session structure, pacing, engagement, clarity, professionalism, and alignment to SI expectations.

Outcome: Actionable coaching points and a follow-up plan (what to keep, what to change, by when).

Start-of-Semester Communication

Email Professor + Request Blackboard Access

  • Introduce yourself as the SI Leader; include lecture days/times.
  • Only applies if professor teaches multiple sections.
  • Request instructor-level access to post announcements and forms.
  • Ask for course-specific preferences (what to cover, handling help).

Selling the Value of SI

Early + Before Major Assessments

  • Remind students SI is a built-in support resource.
  • Sessions are low-pressure practice spaces: questions, building confidence.
  • Clarify weekly expectations (review + practice + exam prep).
  • SI is for everyone, not only for students struggling.
  • Post reminders before exams and big assignments.

Typical Session Focus Areas (Student-Facing)

Review lecture concepts in simpler terms.
Work through practice problems together.
Prepare for upcoming exams and quizzes.
Answer questions on assignments/labs within instructor guidelines.

FERPA: What You Must Always Do

  • Do not share any student information (grades, attendance, performance, identity) with anyone who is not authorized.
  • Do not reveal who attended SI sessions - to other students, friends, family, strangers, and generally not to the professor.
  • Share SI-related student information only through approved channels (APS leadership/program staff) and only when needed for program duties.

Mandatory Reporting

If a student discloses self-harm, harm to others, or serious safety concerns, notify your APS or appropriate university contact immediately per policy.

If cheating/academic integrity issues are disclosed, follow the program’s reporting pathway (APS/program staff and/or instructor as appropriate).

Note: SI Leaders sign FERPA-related acknowledgement each semester; compliance is required.

APS Responsibilities

APS leaders are responsible for the quality, consistency, and growth of SI operations. Your role is both managerial and developmental.

Pre-Semester

  • Assess possible courses for SI support and identify staffing needs.
  • Advertise SI positions and recruit candidates.
  • Interview and select SI Leaders.
  • Hire/onboard and ensure required paperwork and access are completed.
  • Train SI Leaders on session structure, engagement, professionalism, and FERPA.
  • Coordinate with faculty to confirm expectations, access, and communication pathways.

In-Semester

  • Hold weekly APS office hours and be accessible for coaching and questions.
  • Run monthly meetings (training + discussion + program updates).
  • Check journals/lesson plans for completeness and quality; provide feedback.
  • Conduct APS observations early and follow up with coaching plans.
  • Strengthen SI Leaders: share strategies, celebrate wins, and build peer connection.
  • Support attendance growth through messaging strategies and faculty collaboration.
  • Ensure professionalism and FERPA compliance (address issues immediately).

End-of-Semester

  • Ensure ERCs are completed (and encourage exam-by-exam ERCs when feasible).
  • Coordinate peer observations during the last month and ensure forms are completed.
  • Collect outcomes, identify program improvement priorities, and document next steps.
